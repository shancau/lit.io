Coconut Trees

The jeepney’s tired hum eats the road’s cracks—
plastic saints sway, their gold crowns flaking paint.
Lolo’s hands carve stories from storm-warped wood,
while the sea spits back nets full of ghost crabs
and the peso’s weight shrinks like drying fish.
We kneel to coconuts, not crosses.

Roofs sag like Grandma’s spine,
her palms cracked from husking hope
into bowls of rice that never swell.
Back when palm wine dripped sweeter than sermons,
we’d ditch school to chase bike taxis
down roads where resorts rise like tombstones,
“Private” signs nailed where our footprints bled.

But the trees don’t judge.
Their trunks twist like old men’s fists,
roots clawing graves the tides stole.
We climb anyway, skin raw from the hunger
for a sky taller than tin-roofed mornings.

Everybody knows the cop’s cut,
but his smirk gleams brighter than patrol lights.
Coconut trees, they don’t lie—
their shade hides the machete’s kiss on bark,
sap slow as Grandpa’s tales of harvests
that once bought shoes, not just salt.

Now kids scroll dreams on cracked screens,
filming waves for clout, tagging Dubai
while chain saws hum a hymn to the grove’s end.
The priest preaches patience.
The trees just grow.
A dog barks. A knife grins. The sun don’t care.
